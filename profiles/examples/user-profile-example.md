## Who I Am

Alex Chen is a builder and solo founder based in Austin, Texas. Spent 8 years making indie games -- some shipped, some abandoned, all educational. Spent the next 2 years building developer tools after realizing that the problem blocking most indie devs wasn't skill or creativity; it was the production pipeline. Now running Pixel Forge Studios LLC: AI tools for indie game developers who can't afford full teams.

Not going back to a game studio. The constraints there (design by committee, 3-year timelines, one small piece of a large project) were slowly suffocating. This is better.

Background: Grew up in Portland, OR. Studied computer science at UC Davis. Moved to Austin for the tech scene and stayed for the weather and the people. Parents are in the Bay Area.

## Thinking & Processing

- **Thinks by building.** The equivalent of "thinking by talking" -- but the output is a prototype, not words. Ideas get stress-tested by coding them, not by writing about them. This is a feature, not a bug. Ember supports this by reducing the overhead of spinning up experiments.

- **Flow state is the dominant mode.** Long uninterrupted sessions. Loses track of time. Best work happens in 3-4 hour blocks. Biological needs (food, water) are what forces breaks. The agent respects flow state and doesn't interrupt it for non-urgent things.
- **Stepping away produces insight.** Climbing gym, long walks, cooking -- the solutions to hard problems often surface there. Under-uses this deliberately, defaults to grinding instead of stepping back.
- **External structure for non-work commitments.** The gym is on the calendar. Therapy is on the calendar. Social plans need to be on the calendar or they don't happen. Work is self-sustaining. Everything else needs friction removed.

## Failure Modes

Things Ember should watch for and gently flag:

- **Feature creep.** Every new user conversation reveals a new feature that would be "really useful." The backlog grows. The core tool gets diluted. Observable signal: when the sprint backlog has more than 6 items and none of them are related to the core use case. Ember's response: "That's the fourth scope expansion this week. What's the one thing that matters most for the next 30 days?"
- **Over-polishing.** Ships too late because it's not quite right. "I'll launch when the edge cases are handled" is a known trap. Observable signal: a feature that was "almost done" two weeks ago is still in the same state. Ember's response: surface the date it was first marked "almost done" and ask what's actually blocking.
- **Game jam mode bleeding into product work.** The 48-hour jam mindset (ship anything, sleep later, momentum over quality) is great during jams. Destructive applied to a product. Observable signal: commits at 2 AM for three nights in a row, or "I'll just fix this one more thing" after 10 PM. Ember's response: "Jam mode detected. Is there a deadline driving this, or is this just momentum?"
- **Getting lost in jam participation.** Game jams are legitimately useful (user empathy, community, prototype ideas). They're also a way to avoid the harder work of building a business. Observable signal: entered a jam during a week when business priorities were stalled. Ember's response: note it once, don't repeat it.

- **Accessible tools.** The free tier should be real, not a gimmick. Indie devs don't have budgets. If the tool only works for people who can pay, it fails the mission.

- **Quality through iteration.** Ships things that work, then improves them. Not ships perfect things on a long timeline. The game jam instinct applied appropriately.
- **Direct communication.** Doesn't enjoy hedging. If something is broken, say it's broken. If a decision needs to be made, make it. This applies to Ember too.

## Communication Style

- **Visual and demo-oriented.** Explanations land better with screenshots, GIFs, or a quick prototype. When describing an idea, often reaches for "let me just show you." Ember should supplement text with visual examples when they'd help.
- **Short messages with lots of links.** Doesn't write long paragraphs when a link or a screenshot will do. Expects the same from Ember -- the point quickly, then the supporting detail.
- **Occasional voice dictation for longer thoughts.** When processing something complex (strategy, relationship stuff, hard decisions), might dictate stream-of-consciousness. Ember extracts intent from the messy input.
- **Iterates through conversation.** Rarely gives a complete spec upfront. Starts with "I want to do something like X" and refines through dialogue. Ember asks one clarifying question at a time, not a list of five.
- **Platform-adaptive.** Reddit is long-form and technical. Discord is conversational and short. GitHub issues are terse and specific. Doesn't expect Ember to write the same way everywhere.

## Decision-Making

- **Build the prototype, then decide.** The prototype reveals constraints and possibilities that aren't visible in the abstract. "Let's build a quick version" is how Alex starts most decisions.
- **Talk to 3 users before committing.** Feature decisions go through at least 3 conversations with actual users. One person's strong opinion isn't enough. Three people mentioning the same thing is a signal.
- **Revenue as validation signal.** Someone paying is different from someone saying they'd pay. Watches conversion data carefully, not just engagement.
- **Reversibility test.** Reversible? Do it now, learn from it. Hard to reverse? Slow down and think.
- **Clean breaks.** When something isn't working, cut it. Did it with games that weren't finding audiences. Will do it with tools if the data says so.
- **Parallel workstreams when possible.** Product work and content work can run simultaneously. Don't sequence things that don't need to be sequential.

## Life Goals

### Purpose
**Make tools that let indie devs build games they couldn't build alone.**

The daily gut check. Can I point to a game that shipped because of Pixel Forge? That's the filter. As the user base grows, the answer should become "yes" more and more frequently.

### People
**Be genuinely embedded in the indie dev community, not just visible in it.**

Posting content is not the same as belonging. The goal is relationships -- devs whose work I follow, who follow mine, who I'd grab a beer with at GDC. Online-first is fine; depth matters more than location.

### Profit
**Build a profitable tool business that sustains the work and grows from it.**

Revenue is a signal and a sustainability mechanism. The model needs to work. $19/month is the current bet. Whether that's the right price, right tier structure, right product -- that's what 2026 is for finding out.

### Health
**Maintain physical and mental health as a load-bearing foundation, not an afterthought.**

The work is good. The risk is the work crowding out everything else. The gym, therapy, and real relationships are the infrastructure the work runs on. Protect them.
