# Mental Models Reference

Select 1-2 models per decision based on relevance. Not a checklist -- pick what fits.

---

## Thinking

### Inversion
**What:** Instead of "how do I succeed?", ask "how would I guarantee failure?" Then avoid those things.
**When:** Stuck on how to approach a problem. Complex goals with many paths.
**Counter-strategy:** Prevents tunnel vision on one path forward.

### First Principles
**What:** Break a problem down to its fundamental truths, then rebuild reasoning from there. Strip away assumptions and analogies.
**When:** "Everyone does it this way" is the only justification. Conventional wisdom feels wrong.
**Counter-strategy:** Defeats argument from authority and analogy-based reasoning.

### Second-Order Thinking
**What:** "And then what?" -- chase consequences 3-4 levels deep. First-order effects are obvious; second and third order effects are where surprises live.
**When:** Decisions with ripple effects. Policy changes. Community dynamics.
**Counter-strategy:** Prevents short-term optimization that creates long-term problems.

### Map vs. Territory
**What:** The model of reality is not reality. Plans, research, and frameworks are maps -- they simplify and distort.
**When:** Over-relying on research. Analysis paralysis. "The data says..." as a substitute for judgment.
**Counter-strategy:** Keep testing against reality. Ship and see what actually happens.

## Economics

### Opportunity Cost
**What:** Every choice has a hidden cost -- what you gave up by not choosing the alternative. Time spent on X is time NOT spent on Y.
**When:** Resource allocation. "Should I do X or Y?" Especially when both options seem good.
**Counter-strategy:** Forces explicit comparison instead of evaluating options in isolation.

### Sunk Cost Fallacy
**What:** Past investment (time, money, emotion) should NOT influence future decisions. Only future costs and benefits matter.
**When:** "But we already..." appears in reasoning. Reluctance to abandon something that isn't working.
**Counter-strategy:** Ask: "If I were starting fresh today with no history, would I choose this?"

### Margin of Safety
**What:** Build buffers. If something needs to work at 100 units, design for 130. Accounts for uncertainty and unknown unknowns.
**When:** Estimates, timelines, financial projections. Anything where being wrong has real cost.
**Counter-strategy:** Prevents overconfidence in point estimates.

## Psychology

### Anchoring
**What:** The first piece of information disproportionately influences all subsequent judgment. First price, first option, first impression.
**When:** Evaluating options presented in sequence. Negotiation. Any decision where you saw one option first.
**Counter-strategy:** Deliberately re-evaluate starting from the last option considered. Generate your own anchor before looking at external ones.

### Loss Aversion
**What:** Losses feel ~2x worse than equivalent gains feel good. People will take irrational risks to avoid losses.
**When:** "What if I lose X?" dominates thinking. Risk of change feels scarier than risk of stagnation.
**Counter-strategy:** Reframe: "What's the cost of NOT changing over 12 months?" Make the status quo loss visible.

### Social Proof
**What:** "Everyone else is doing it" feels like evidence. It isn't -- it's comfort.
**When:** Justifying a choice because others made it. Community consensus as a decision input.
**Counter-strategy:** Ask: "Would this be the right choice if nobody else were doing it?"

### Confirmation Bias
**What:** We seek, interpret, and remember information that confirms what we already believe.
**When:** Research conveniently supports initial hunch. Dismissing contradictory evidence as "edge cases."
**Counter-strategy:** Actively search for disconfirming evidence. Assign someone to argue the opposite case.

### Status Quo Bias
**What:** "Keep things as they are" feels safer than any change, regardless of whether the current state is good.
**When:** Default is inaction. "It's fine" as a reason to not improve. Reluctance to switch tools/approaches.
**Counter-strategy:** "If I were starting fresh, would I choose the current setup?" If no, the status quo is a choice too.

## Systems

### Feedback Loops
**What:** Outputs of a system become inputs. Positive loops amplify (growth or collapse). Negative loops stabilize.
**When:** Understanding why something accelerated or stalled. Community dynamics. Business growth patterns.
**Counter-strategy:** Identify which loops are running and whether they're helping or hurting.

### Bottleneck Analysis
**What:** The system's throughput is limited by its narrowest constraint. Optimizing non-bottlenecks is waste.
**When:** "I need to do more of everything." Feeling overwhelmed. Resource allocation.
**Counter-strategy:** Find the ONE thing that, if improved, would move the whole system. Ignore the rest.

### Leverage Points
**What:** Small changes in the right place produce large systemic effects. Not all interventions are equal.
**When:** Limited resources. Choosing where to invest time/energy. System redesign.
**Counter-strategy:** Instead of "what can I change?", ask "where would a small change have the biggest effect?"

## Strategy

### Reversibility
**What:** Two-way doors (reversible) -> move fast, decide now. One-way doors (irreversible) -> slow down, gather more info.
**When:** Every decision. This is the first filter.
**Counter-strategy:** Prevents both over-analysis of trivial choices and under-analysis of permanent ones.

### Information-Revelation
**What:** Decisions are like poker hands. Deploy small experiments to reveal information, then make bigger bets with better data.
**When:** High uncertainty. "I don't know enough to decide." Market validation. Testing assumptions.
**Counter-strategy:** Turns "big scary decision" into "small experiment that tells me what to do next."

### Pre-Mortem
**What:** "It's 1 year from now. This failed completely. Write the post-mortem." Surfaces risks that optimism hides.
**When:** High-stakes decisions. Plans that feel too clean. Unanimous agreement (suspicious).
**Counter-strategy:** Forces concrete failure scenarios instead of vague "risks."

### Regret Minimization
**What:** "When I'm 80, which choice will I regret more -- doing this and it failing, or never trying?"
**When:** Career decisions. Life direction. When fear of failure is the dominant emotion.
**Counter-strategy:** Cuts through short-term anxiety to reveal long-term values.
